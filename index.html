SLP + LLM
Field Guide

A practical, clinician-first resource for speech-language pathologists navigating language models in clinical and educational settings.

"AI should reduce cognitive load, not replace clinical judgment."

Why "I/O"?  In computing, I/O means input/output — how a system receives and sends information. In speech-language pathology, that's what we do every day: we study how people take in language and produce it. SLP/IO sits at that intersection — where clinical communication meets the tools that process it.

Origin

Why This Exists

SLP/IO started with a simple observation: clinicians are already using language models, but nobody in the field was talking about how to do it well.

The Gap

SLPs work at the intersection of language, cognition, and communication—exactly the domains LLMs operate in. Yet the field had no framework for evaluating or integrating these tools responsibly.

The Background

This resource is built by a clinician who also builds software—iOS apps, web platforms, audio signal processing tools, and AAC systems. That dual lens drives everything here.

The Problem

Without guidance, clinicians either avoid AI entirely or adopt it uncritically. Both paths carry risk. One leaves efficiency on the table; the other threatens quality and compliance.

The Position

SLP/IO isn't pro-AI or anti-AI. It's pro-clinician. The goal is to strengthen clinical thinking with better tools, not to replace it with faster ones.

Harmonic Systems is the studio behind SLP/IO. It represents a body of work spanning voice science, accessible communication technology, and applied AI—all built from the perspective that the best tools emerge when clinical expertise and engineering work together. SLP/IO is one expression of that philosophy: a living resource where the rigor of speech-language pathology meets the capability of modern language models.
Foundation

Core Principles

Not rules imposed from outside the field. These reflect how thoughtful clinicians already think about tools, documentation, and professional responsibility.

01
AI supports thinking; it does not replace judgment.

Language models can help organize, structure, and refine clinical writing. The clinician remains responsible for meaning, accuracy, and individualization.

02
Never include PHI in public tools.

Compliance with HIPAA and FERPA is non-negotiable. No student names, DOBs, school names, or identifiable details in any public LLM.

03
Outputs must always be reviewed.

No LLM output should enter an IEP, progress note, or legal document without professional review and revision.

04
Better documentation should still sound human.

Polished language is not the same as good clinical writing. If a note sounds generic, it probably is.

05
Evidence-based practice ≠ polished wording.

A well-formatted paragraph can still contain vague goals, weak measurement criteria, or generic descriptions that fail the student.

06
Precision in language is not pedantry.

Subtle shifts in wording can change clinical intent. Specificity protects students and their access to services.

Practice

Use Cases & Red Flags

Language models are most useful where documentation is structured and repetitive. They are weakest where individualization, interpretation, and context matter most.

Appropriate Use

  • ✓ Structuring and organizing progress notes
  • ✓ Brainstorming goal wording (with clinician editing)
  • ✓ Drafting parent-friendly explanations
  • ✓ Simplifying complex professional language
  • ✓ Organizing treatment session ideas
  • ✓ Rewriting content for clarity
  • ✓ Structured ethical reflection

Red Flags

  • ✗ Entering identifiable student info into public models
  • ✗ Copying AI outputs directly into IEPs or legal docs
  • ✗ Using AI for diagnostic or eligibility decisions
  • ✗ Replacing supervision or clinical consultation
  • ✗ Letting fluent language disguise weak reasoning
  • ✗ Confusing convenience with competence
  • ✗ Polished wording hiding non-measurable goals
Tools

Prompt Templates

Starting points, not copy-paste solutions. Each template preserves your clinical voice while reducing the cognitive load of documentation tasks.

Documentation

Progress Note Organizer

Turn messy session notes into structured documentation.

I am an SLP. Here are my raw session notes for a student (no identifying info included). Help me organize these into a structured progress note with: target skill, level of support, performance data, context, and clinical interpretation. Do not add details I did not provide.
Communication

Parent Email Draft

Rewrite clinical information in accessible, parent-friendly language.

I need to communicate the following clinical information to a parent in clear, supportive, non-jargon language. Maintain accuracy but make it readable for a non-specialist. Here is the information: [paste your content]
IEP Quality

Goal Clarity Check

Evaluate whether a goal meets measurability and individualization standards.

Review this IEP goal and tell me: (1) Is it observable? (2) Is it measurable with clear criteria? (3) Does it specify conditions and supports? (4) Could another clinician act on it immediately? If any answer is no, suggest a revision. Goal: [paste goal]
Ethics

Ethical Reflection

Think through a clinical situation more clearly without outsourcing the decision.

I am an SLP working in a school setting. I am dealing with a situation involving [general scenario, no identifying info]. Help me think through: relevant ethical considerations, possible risks, and factors I may be overlooking. Do not give direct instructions or definitive answers. Focus on helping me reflect more clearly.
Communication

Jargon Simplifier

Translate clinical or technical language for broader audiences.

Rewrite the following clinical text so it is understandable to someone without a clinical background. Keep the meaning accurate but use plain language. Do not oversimplify in ways that change the clinical intent. Text: [paste text]
Research

What the Evidence Says

The evidence base for LLMs in clinical documentation is growing, but still early. Here is an honest summary across disciplines.

Supported

  • LLMs improve documentation efficiency and reduce time spent writing notes
  • Outputs tend to be more organized, structured, and readable
  • Strongest results when used as a "second pass" tool: revising, summarizing, restructuring
  • Emerging evidence for detecting biased or stigmatizing language in clinical notes
  • Cross-disciplinary consistency in medicine, OT, and emerging SLP applications

Not Supported

  • Independent note writing without clinician review
  • Diagnostic or eligibility decisions
  • Handling sensitive data in public tools (violates HIPAA/FERPA)
  • Replacing clinical voice with generic polished language
  • Factual accuracy without verification (hallucination risk remains real)
Bottom line: LLMs perform best as documentation copilots, not independent authors. They are strongest at organizing, refining, and structuring. They are weakest at interpreting, individualizing, and deciding.

Evolving Landscape

Digital Media Literacy

The concept of "media" has changed faster than most professional frameworks can track. For SLPs, this isn't an abstract concern — it directly affects how our students communicate, how we assess communication, and what "literate" even means.

How the Medium Has Shifted

Pre-2010

Media = broadcast, print, static

Communication was largely one-directional. Literacy meant reading, writing, and understanding published content. Media was something you consumed.

2010 – 2018

Media = social, participatory, multimodal

Everyone became a publisher. Communication shifted to short-form, image-heavy, and conversational. Students began learning pragmatics partly through screens.

2018 – 2023

Media = algorithmic, curated, ambient

Feeds became personalized. Voice assistants normalized human-machine dialogue. AAC technology started converging with mainstream tools. The line between "assistive" and "everyday" tech blurred.

2023 – Present

Media = generative, synthetic, co-authored

Language models produce fluent text on demand. AI-generated images and speech are indistinguishable from human-created content. The question shifts from "who wrote this" to "what process produced this."

Emerging

Media = embodied, adaptive, voice-native

Real-time voice interfaces, ambient computing, and adaptive systems are reshaping how humans interact with information. Communication is becoming continuous, multimodal, and deeply personal.

Why This Matters for SLPs

Voice is the New Interface

Voice assistants, real-time transcription, and speech-to-text are normalizing spoken interaction with systems. SLPs understand vocal production, prosody, and intent in ways no other profession does.

Fluency ≠ Understanding

LLMs produce fluent text without comprehension. Students (and adults) may confuse polished AI output with verified information. Teaching the difference between generated language and grounded communication is now a literacy skill.

Accessibility Is Converging

AAC, screen readers, captioning, and voice interfaces are merging with mainstream technology. The tools our students depend on are becoming the tools everyone uses. That's an opportunity and a responsibility.

Documentation Is Changing

Clinical documentation now exists alongside AI-generated content, auto-summaries, and ambient scribes. Maintaining authenticity, precision, and clinical voice in this environment requires new awareness.

The SLP position: We are not just consumers of media or users of technology. We are professionals trained in the mechanics of human communication. As the medium shifts, our expertise becomes more relevant, not less. Digital media literacy isn't a side topic for our field — it's an extension of what we've always done: helping people communicate clearly, honestly, and effectively in whatever environment they inhabit.

Background

About This Resource

SLP/IO is a Harmonic Systems project—built by a speech-language pathologist and software developer who has spent years working at the intersection of clinical practice, voice science, and technology.

The Harmonic Systems portfolio includes over 130 projects: iOS apps for voice therapy and AAC, Swift frameworks for audio signal processing, web platforms for clinical education, and tools that bridge the gap between acoustic measurement and clinical meaning.

SLP/IO exists because clinicians are already using language models informally, often without a framework for doing so safely or effectively. The goal is not to promote AI adoption. It's to define what responsible use looks like in a field where language carries legal and clinical weight.

These tools are entering clinical workflows whether we're ready or not. The question is not whether to use them, but how to use them in a way that strengthens—not weakens—clinical thinking.

Transparency note: This resource was developed with the assistance of language models to help organize and refine content. All material has been reviewed and shaped through clinical judgment and is intended to support — not replace — professional decision-making.
